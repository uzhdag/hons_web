      Welcome!

      Networks, mathematically formalized as graphs, have become a ubiquitous abstraction of complex systems. To extract knowledge from data on such systems, the network science community has built a suite of algorithms, software packages, and visualization techniques. While the challenges posed by increasingly large datasets drive much of the research in data mining, graph mining and social network analysis, in this satellite we focus on the challenges created by increasingly complex data.

      Being the continuation of successful editions held in Berkeley, Zaragoza, Seoul, and Indanapolis, and Paris this satellite will be held at NetSci 2019 in Burlington, Vermont, USA. Continuing the tradition of previous years, the 2019 edition in Burlington, Vermont will again feature a specific focus track on a topic that - according to the literature of the past year - is of particular interest for the network science community. Following last year's focus on the relation between non-Markovian and non-dyadic higher-order models, the 2019 edition will dedicate a track to the open challenge of learning and model selection, i.e. methods that allow us to find and/or select the "optimal" higher-order model to analyze a given data set. Through this focus, we hope to create a link to the machine learning community, which is interested in similar questions in the context of statistical learning and predictive modelling. The different disciplinary backgrounds of the organizers (physics, mathematics, computer science, data science) will further help us to maintain the cross-disciplinary spirit of previous HONS editions.
